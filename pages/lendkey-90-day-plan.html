How I'm reading the role

Three things are true at once, and the plan is shaped around them.

1
Sigma is the strategic platform
End-to-end ownership: admin, stakeholder partnership, dashboard strategy, KPI consistency, embedded lender reporting. Sigma is where the business goes for data.
2
A legacy estate has to move
PowerBI + SSRS reports are still serving the business and must land in Sigma without breaking trust. Migration backlog has to be prioritized, sequenced, and tracked.
3
Two adjacent systems matter
Taktile (decisioning) gets a structured ramp; Databricks SQL is the daily tool. ETL/pipeline reliability supports both. None of these can be neglected.
4
PDF lender reports are the lifeline
Reconciliation, performance, and compliance call reports go to external lenders. They cannot regress while the bigger migration is in flight.

Operating principles for the first 90 days

Defaults that shape every decision in the plan below.

  • Listen before I build. No new dashboard in week one. Stakeholder interviews and existing-state audits come first; building on a wrong mental model is the most expensive mistake in a BI role.
  • The lender reports cannot regress. External-facing PDF reports get a stability contract on day one. Anything I change there is dual-controlled and reconciled to the prior cycle.
  • One KPI, one definition, one source. Migration is the moment to retire conflicting definitions. Every metric that moves to Sigma carries a documented owner, source query, and acceptance criteria.
  • Migrate by value, not by alphabet. The backlog is sequenced by user count × business criticality × migration cost — not by ticket order. The Director of Analytics signs off on the ranking.
  • Adoption is a deliverable. A dashboard nobody uses is debt. Each migrated report ships with a short demo, a Loom or doc, and a named user who confirms it replaces the old artifact.
  • Trust is built on documentation. Public KPI dictionary, migration inventory, and a "what's in Sigma" landing page from week four onward. Nothing private to my head.

Phase plan · what gets done, week by week

Each phase has a theme, a working set, and an exit gate I can be measured against.

Days 1–30 · Listen & audit

Earn the right to build by understanding what's already there.

No new dashboards. The goal is a complete, written picture of the Sigma estate, the migration backlog, the PDF lender reports, and the Taktile flows — plus a stakeholder map I can act on.

Sigma platform audit

  • Inventory every workbook, dataset, and connection in Sigma; tag by owner, audience, criticality, last-edited.
  • Review Sigma admin posture: workspaces, permissions, row-level security, source connections to Databricks.
  • Document the current dashboard taxonomy and where it leaks (orphaned reports, duplicate metrics).

Stakeholder discovery

  • 30-minute interviews with every stakeholder: lending ops, servicing, compliance, finance, exec sponsors, and external lender contacts.
  • Three questions each: what report do you actually use? what decision does it drive? what's broken about it?
  • Standing 1:1 cadence with the Director of Analytics; alignment on success criteria for the 90 days.

Legacy migration inventory

  • Catalog every PowerBI and SSRS report — source, owner, audience, refresh frequency, current SLA.
  • Rank-order candidates: user count × business criticality × technical migration cost.
  • Identify the "do not migrate, retire" list and get sign-off from owners before they take effort.

Lender PDF reports — stability contract

  • Map the reconciliation, performance, and compliance call report pipelines end-to-end (source → transform → render → delivery).
  • Document delivery SLAs, recipients, and the exact reconciliation checks that must pass.
  • Stand up a "no-regression" baseline so any change ships with a prior-cycle diff.

Taktile & Databricks ramp

  • Walk every existing Taktile flow with the current owner; take handoff notes; shadow one decision in production.
  • Map Databricks workspaces, schemas, and the canonical tables that power lending reporting.
  • Validate ETL job ownership and the on-call story for pipeline failures.
Exit gate (day 30): written Sigma audit, ranked migration backlog approved by the Director of Analytics, lender-report stability contract, Taktile shadowing complete, Databricks SQL fluency demonstrated on at least one production query.
Days 31–60 · Build & migrate

Ship the first wave — and set the patterns the rest of the estate will follow.

Migration is the moment to standardize. The first 3–5 migrations establish naming, KPI definitions, layout, access patterns, and the documentation surface. Everything that comes later inherits from this.

KPI standardization in Sigma

  • Publish a versioned KPI dictionary: every metric carries an owner, source query, granularity, refresh, and acceptance test.
  • Build a reusable Sigma dataset layer — semantic objects, not one-off SQL pasted into each workbook.
  • Establish naming, color, and layout conventions; commit a Sigma style guide.

First migration wave (3–5 reports)

  • Sequence by value: the top of the ranked backlog. Mix of "high user count, low complexity" and "single high-stakes report."
  • Each migration includes a parallel-run window — old and new produced for a full cycle, deltas explained, sign-off documented.
  • No migration ships without a named business owner confirming it replaces the legacy artifact.

Lender PDF reports — own them

  • Take direct ownership of reconciliation, performance, and compliance call reports.
  • Refactor the generation pipeline where appropriate; Python automation where it reduces manual touch without adding fragility.
  • Begin the design conversation for embedded lender reporting in Sigma (architecture sketch, security model, lender-by-lender access).

Taktile — first co-owned flow

  • Take co-ownership of one existing flow with the current owner; handle changes end-to-end under review.
  • Document the flow inventory, change procedure, and rollback story.
  • Begin scoping the next flow modification or new flow with product/risk.

Documentation surface

  • "What's in Sigma" landing page — entry point for every business user, organized by domain.
  • Migration inventory — what's migrated, what's pending, what's been retired, with status and owner.
  • Decision log — every KPI redefinition, every report retirement, every dashboard pattern change.
Exit gate (day 60): 3–5 reports live in Sigma with parallel-run sign-off, KPI dictionary v1 published, lender PDF reports owned without regression, one Taktile flow co-owned with documented change history, public docs surface in place.
Days 61–90 · Scale & own

Become the owner the role asks for — adoption, roadmap, and platform thinking.

The work shifts from "ship the first wave" to "make Sigma the obvious default." Adoption gets a marketing program; the migration moves into steady cadence; the embedded-lender vision gets a concrete POC; the Director of Analytics gets a Q-by-Q roadmap.

Adoption program

  • Weekly Sigma office hours; quarterly "what's new" digest; per-domain training sessions for ops, servicing, compliance.
  • Track and publish adoption metrics: active users, time-to-first-report, top dashboards, dashboards with zero views.
  • Position BI as a self-service resource — guided templates, copy-able starter workbooks, a "request a dashboard" workflow.

Migration cadence

  • Sustainable weekly migration rate; backlog visible to stakeholders; retirements as routine as new dashboards.
  • Next 5–10 reports queued and scoped; first PowerBI/SSRS reports formally decommissioned.
  • Migration framework documented well enough that a future analyst could continue without me.

Embedded lender reporting POC

  • Architecture proposal for embedded lender reporting in Sigma — security model, lender isolation, branding, performance.
  • Working POC against a single representative lender; review with product, security, and an external lender contact.
  • Roadmap delta: what it would take to move from POC to GA across the lender book.

Taktile — independent ownership

  • Independent maintenance of at least one production flow.
  • Lead a new-flow scoping conversation with risk/product; ship a small flow change end-to-end under review.
  • Document flow patterns and the decision-rationale archive for future flows.

Q-by-Q reporting roadmap

  • Present a 4-quarter reporting roadmap to the Director of Analytics — migration completion, embedded lender reporting, Taktile scope, ETL/pipeline investments.
  • Risk register, dependency map, and resourcing asks attached.
  • Explicit success metrics for the next 90 days, so this plan keeps cycling.
Exit gate (day 90): adoption metrics being tracked and trending up, sustainable migration cadence with first decommissions executed, embedded lender reporting POC reviewed with stakeholders, independent Taktile ownership demonstrated, Q-by-Q roadmap approved by the Director of Analytics.

Agentic automation layer · the analyst force-multiplier I'd build

The migration is the visible work. The bigger leverage is in what gets automated around it. Six role-specialized agents — modeled after the Cowork autonomous pipeline I run for myself today — that take the routine load off the analyst seat and surface only the decisions that need a human.

Pattern reference · proven build

This isn't theoretical — I run a 16-agent autonomous pipeline in production today.

Risks I'm watching, and how I'd de-risk them

The plan is opinionated; that means it's wrong in places I can't see yet. These are the most plausible failure modes and what I'd do early to defuse them.

Sigma is new to me; I overestimate transfer from Power BI

Sigma's data modeling, governance, and embedded-analytics model have their own conventions. Pattern transfer from Power BI/Looker covers most cases but not all.

De-risk: Sigma certification track in week one; peer review on the first three migrations from someone who's been on Sigma longer; explicit "Sigma-isms" notes in the style guide.

Lender PDF reports regress during migration

These reports leave the building. A reconciliation miss is visible to the lender immediately and erodes trust quickly.

De-risk: stability contract on day one, prior-cycle diff on every change, no Sigma-driven changes to lender PDFs in the first 30 days, dual-control on the generation pipeline.

Migration backlog gets prioritized by squeaky wheel

Whoever asks loudest tends to jump the queue. That breaks the value-ranked sequence and lengthens the overall migration.

De-risk: public ranked backlog, explicit ranking criteria, the Director of Analytics owns the ranking, escalations go through the roadmap conversation — not the inbox.

Taktile ramp competes with migration for attention

Taktile is a different cognitive load (rules & decisioning, not BI). Splitting context costs more than it looks like on paper.

De-risk: fixed Taktile time blocks each week, written handoff doc from the current owner, scope creep on flow changes refused until Phase 3.

ETL/pipeline issues consume the calendar

If pipelines are fragile, firefighting eats the migration plan. I've seen this happen in regulated reporting environments.

De-risk: Phase 1 pipeline-reliability triage; agreement with the Director of Analytics on what's in scope vs. owned by data engineering; alerting baseline before the migration accelerates.

Adoption never materializes

A migrated dashboard nobody opens is technical debt with a fresh coat of paint.

De-risk: named business owner on every migration, parallel-run sign-off, adoption metrics tracked from Phase 2, office hours start the moment the first dashboards land.

Why this plan, why LendKey

The role and the company are unusually well-matched to my background, and the plan reflects that.

Mission alignment

LendKey's model — community banks and credit unions reaching national markets through a digital platform — is exactly the kind of mission-aligned fintech where I've spent the last seven years. Federally regulated student lending at Sallie Mae taught me what it costs to get reporting wrong in this environment, and what it earns to get it right.

Operating style

I work the way the JD describes the role: independently, against competing priorities, with stakeholders rather than just tickets. The portfolio is a record of that — case studies that show problem framing and tradeoffs, not just screenshots; full-stack work shipped end-to-end; adoption treated as a real deliverable.

Honest about ramp areas

Sigma and Taktile are new to me; Databricks SQL is adjacent to my deep Snowflake background. The plan treats these as ramp areas with explicit structure — certification, shadowing, co-ownership — rather than pretending the transfer is zero-cost.

What I'd want from LendKey
  • The Director of Analytics's time for weekly 1:1s in the first 30 days.
  • Access to Sigma admin, Databricks, Taktile, and a few representative lender PDFs by day three.
  • Permission to talk to one external lender contact in Phase 1, and one in Phase 3.
  • An explicit "no" on at least two requests in Phase 2, so the migration sequence holds.
